Reasearch Question:¶

In what ways have housing prices in Boulder changed over the last ten years, and has that change led to or been driven by a change in the income of the population living in the boulder area?

My motivation for this quesiton is simple. On the r/Boulder subreddit there are numerous posts about the cost of living and also a large number of real estate related posts. The majority of the posts about the cost of living in boulder is complaints in the increase in housing prices, and how people are being driven from their lifetime homes by gentrificaiton. The posts about real estate are commentary about the increase in prices in boulder. I am interested in seeing if there is a corelation. I was origionaly looking at the homeless population but there is no data in boulder about the homless population outside of just how many people spend time at a shelter.

This question is important because it affects peoples lives. The cost of living in Boulder is dramatically higher than the national average, and the cost of renting is also extreamaly high. https://www.bestplaces.net/cost_of_living/city/colorado/boulder

Hypothosis¶

My hypothosis is that the data will show a positive relaitonship between the income increase in boulder, and the increase in housing prices in boulder, as opposed to an inverse relationship.

This is my hypothosis because it is fairly easy to assume that only people with a high enough income can afford an expensive house. As housing prices rise, people who cannot afford the price will not move into boulder, but people who can, will. vice versa, if people with a higher income are moving to boulder, there will be a demand for higher quality and more expensive housing in the boulder county area, leading to a higher average housing price.

Conclusion¶

My hypothosis was suported by the data. It is not a statisticaly tested corralational relationship, but as expected, there is a trend towards higher housing prices that occurs over the same effective time frame as an increase in individual and household income in Boulder. This fits with what I have heard about happening in Boulder on reddit and through anecdodal evidence. I also found it unsuprising that there was a increase in mean income for the top income bracket, and a decrease in the mean income for the bottom income bracket. It makes sense with the information I have been hearing about how "The rich get richer while the poor get poorer".

The implication of this is that even though the average income of people in boulder is increasing noticably, it is not a uniform increase. The mean is increasing in spite of the lowest income, not because of them. The limitations to my data were pretty vast. For one, publicly avaliable real estate data was very scarce, and the information that was avaliable was not always what I needed. I went through about three data sets that I scrapped because they were unusable for my purposes, before finding the ones that I could use. Personal income data was also very hard to find, untill I stumbled upon it a few days before this project was due. I re-formated and changed my reshaping along the way in order to achive a more manageable set of information.

Another limitation is that this data can tell me what is happening, but not neccesarily why. I can use it to support my argument, and corrilate the cause and effect, but cannot use it to prove anything is happening one way or another. That is true even if I were to conduct a corrilational t-test or any other statistical test.
