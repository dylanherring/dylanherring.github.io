 Winnowing

        The data I am using comes from: https://www.patriotsoftware.com/blog/accounting/average-cost-living-by-state/. The data is number heavy, and not very expansive. I selected the dataset specifically to look at cost of rent vs income across the united states. There is a potential for bias in where the averages come from, but since the data is all collected in the same way, any variance from reality should be universal across the dataset. The relationship between the datapoints should be the same, so even if every number is skewed high, the relative distance between the two points should still be the same, allowing for an accurate ranking. There is no way to confirm this 100% though.

Discover

        The first task that my prototype should perform is to rank the states from best to worst depending on a chosen filter. Why? So that the user can investigate comparisons between the options easily in order to inform a decision. How? The user can select one of the three categories, and the order of the states will change to reflect the best state in that category. What? This task seaks to learn what the most desirable state to live in is based on a specific category. Where? The task operates on a website, where the data that is displayed is drawn from the dataset depending on how the user selects a filter. When? During the investigation stage of moving to a new area.. Who? The user, specifically someone who wants to look at where they can afford to live.

        The second task I want my prototype to do is to make a recommendation. The user should be able to enter a value for income, and receive a list of states that have a median income equal too or less than that entered value. The tool should also display the data for the average rent in those states. Why? So that the user can see areas where their income would be at or above average, so that they know they can afford to live there. How? The user enters a value, and receives a list of applicable states that meet the criteria. What? This task seeks to learn what states a particular income would be considered at or above average. Where? Also on the website. When? During the investigation stage of moving to a new area. Who? People who are looking to move and want to see where they could move that would be affordable.

Design

        This first sketch does not contain a graphical representation of the data. My initial idea centered on how to communicate the raw data in a digestible format. In my experience, it is essential to have numerical data available in its raw form, but presented in a digestible format. Looking at the prototype, I feel like I have created something that looks strikingly similar to the output of a mortgage calculator website. I like this design, but it is really the bare bones. I want to have a visual graph that reflects the same data. I think both forms can be useful. The interactive graph I created in assignment two could be a good decision to add in the next iteration. The reason I intend to keep the tables in the design is because when it comes to a ranking system, nothing is simpler than a list that starts with the best match, and descends to the worst.

Deploy

        The tasks that I outlined in step three are also visible in the visualizations above. I used an example income of $47,820 for the recommendation task, and I used “Value of a dollar” for the ranking task. The first observation that I made is that Mississippi has the highest value of a dollar. This means that living in Mississippi, you would have the highest purchasing power per dollar out of all the states. This is an interesting discovery because you can also see that Mississippi has a relatively low annual income. This is because there is an inverse relationship between income and purchasing power by state. The second thing I observed was that for the recommendation section, I didn’t actually give the code any kind of ranking instructions, so the recommendations came out in alphabetical order. A future design change would be to give the user the option to order the recommendations the same way they can order the main dataset, by clicking on the column and having it sort by that column in descending order.

Reflect 2

The solution to my problem tells me that designing visualizations for the audience is very difficult. The issue I ran into once I was neck deep in the project was that representing numerical relationships visually can become very misleading and confusing for the user. As a data scientist, I was more than comfortable with the complex graphs that I could have used, but when I got down to it, I realized there was a reason that money related tools on banking sites show you financial information in a table. They don’t want to confuse or mislead customers on accident, so they show them the numbers as numbers, not as colorful datapoints. That influenced my design choices, and I tried to take a realistic approach to the assignment. I wanted to create something that I could imagine presenting at an ideas meeting, where it was visually pleasing, but still realistically adoptable by an actual company.
